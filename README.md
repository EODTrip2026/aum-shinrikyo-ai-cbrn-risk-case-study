# Aum Shinrikyo and AI-Enabled CBRN Risk

This repository contains an independent case study on Aum Shinrikyo and AI-enabled chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear risk.  The paper examines how modern AI systems could affect the pathway from extremist intent to operational CBRN capability.

The purpose of this project is not to argue that AI automatically creates a successful CBRN actor.  Instead, it explores how frontier AI systems may reduce friction across different stages of a threat pathway, including planning, acquisition, troubleshooting, experimentation, and operational learning.

## Project Focus

This case study focuses on three core questions:

1.  How did Aum Shinrikyo move from intent to attempted CBRN capability?
2.  Where did the group face technical, organizational, and operational friction?
3.  How might modern AI systems change similar friction points for future threat actors?

The analysis is written from the perspective of a CBRN/WMD practitioner transitioning into AI safety research.  It is intended to support discussion around frontier model misuse, CBRN uplift, and practical evaluation design.

## Relevance to AI Safety

This project is relevant to AI safety because many misuse risks do not stem from a model providing a single dangerous answer.  Real-world capability often depends on a chain of actions: understanding technical information, selecting feasible approaches, solving practical problems, acquiring materials, interpreting failed attempts, and adapting over time.

The case study uses Aum Shinrikyo as a historical example to examine how AI systems could reduce barriers across that chain.  This framing may be useful for thinking about model evaluations, dangerous capability thresholds, and the kinds of assistance that matter most in high-consequence domains.

## Safety Note

This is a safety-oriented research project.  It does not provide operational CBRN guidance, instructions, recipes, procurement pathways, or tactical advice.  The goal is to better understand misuse pathways so that AI safety researchers, policymakers, and security practitioners can design stronger safeguards.
